The North Channel in High Summer: Killarney to Baie Fine

Margaret Calloway

Cruising Correspondent, Harbour & Sea

The approach to Killarney from the south is one of the great pieces of navigation on the Canadian inland seas. You commit to the channel between Beaverstone Bay and the Collins Inlet approach, and then the water does the rest — it funnels you through, barely a boat-length of clearance on either side at places, the pink granite walls of the Precambrian Shield close enough to read the lichen.

We arrived on a Tuesday with a northwest wind behind us, having left Midland on Saturday morning. The passage north through the open bay had been uneventful — two days of reaching in fifteen knots, the boat moving easily, the crew settled into watch routines. The North Channel begins, technically, at the Strait of Mississagi, but you feel it earlier. The water changes colour. The chart density increases. You start planning three moves ahead.

Killarney is where most Channel cruisers take stock. It has two marinas, a fuel dock, a restaurant where the fish is local, and — crucially — a pay phone outside the government dock that actually works. We stayed two nights, took on water, and studied the pilot charts for the week ahead.

"The North Channel asks something of you. It is not difficult sailing — the distances are short, the anchorages plentiful — but it pays attention to whether you are paying attention."

The Passage to Baie Fine

Baie Fine is thirteen miles east of Killarney by the most direct route. The chart shows it as a narrow finger of water running almost due south into the Shield, terminating in a pool that the pilots call the Pool — as if there is only one. There is, in a sense. You anchor in eight to twelve feet of water surrounded by hills that rise four hundred feet from the waterline on both sides, and the silence is complete.

We anchored bow-to on a Saturday evening. Four other boats were already in the Pool, anchored fore-and-aft in the tradition of the place, their anchor lines stretching to the rock faces where iron rings have been bolted since the Second World War. We followed the convention, dropped the hook, backed toward the eastern wall, and tied off a spring line to a ring that someone put there seventy years ago for exactly this purpose.

THE POOL, BAIE FINE — NORTH CHANNEL

On Watching the Weather

The Channel is open to the northwest in most of its length. The fetch from Sault Ste. Marie is three hundred miles of open water. On the morning we left Baie Fine, the Environment Canada forecast was calling for northwest fifteen to twenty-five, waves one to one-point-five metres on the open water. We left at six, made the narrows at Benjamin Island by eight, and by nine were on a full broad reach at hull speed in eighteen knots of true wind. The wave height on the open water was closer to two metres. The crew ate sandwiches.
