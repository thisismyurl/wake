The North Channel in High Summer

Margaret Calloway

Cruising Correspondent

The approach to Killarney from the south is one of the great pieces of navigation on the Canadian inland seas. You commit to the channel between Beaverstone Bay and the Collins Inlet approach, and then the water does the rest — it funnels you through, barely a boat-length of clearance on either side at places, the pink granite walls of the Precambrian Shield close enough to read the lichen.

We arrived on a Tuesday with a northwest wind behind us, having left Midland on Saturday morning. The passage north through the open bay had been uneventful — two days of reaching in fifteen knots, the boat moving easily, the crew settled into watch routines.

Killarney is where most Channel cruisers take stock. It has two marinas, a fuel dock, a restaurant where the fish is local, and a pay phone outside the government dock that actually works. We stayed two nights, took on water, and studied the pilot charts for the week ahead.

"The North Channel asks something of you. It is not difficult sailing — the distances are short, the anchorages plentiful — but it pays attention to whether you are paying attention."

The Passage to Baie Fine

Baie Fine is thirteen miles east of Killarney by the most direct route. The chart shows it as a narrow finger of water running almost due south into the Shield, terminating in a pool that the pilots call the Pool. You anchor in eight to twelve feet of water surrounded by hills that rise four hundred feet from the waterline on both sides, and the silence is complete.

THE POOL, BAIE FINE

We followed the convention, dropped the hook, backed toward the eastern wall, and tied off a spring line to a ring that someone put there seventy years ago for exactly this purpose.
